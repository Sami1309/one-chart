The Paper

This visualization is based on "All Objects and Some Questions" by Charles H. Lineweaver and Vihan M. Patel, published in the American Journal of Physics 91, 819–825 (2023). The paper charts every known object in the universe by their mass and size.

Two fundamental boundaries carve out forbidden regions: general relativity, which forbids objects smaller than their Schwarzschild radius1 (the black hole line), and quantum mechanics, which forbids localizing a particle below its Compton wavelength2. These boundaries intersect at exactly one point: the Planck-mass instanton3, the smallest possible object and a candidate for the initial conditions of the Universe.

This chart covers all currently known physics as it relates to our understanding of the universe. Current physical understanding cannot cover the forbidden realms partially because they are impossible to measure, by definition.

Key Physics

The Schwarzschild boundary

rs = 2Gm/c². No object can be compressed below its Schwarzschild radius without becoming a black hole. All black holes lie on this diagonal line (slope +1 in log-log space). The region above it is forbidden by gravity.

The Compton boundary

λc = ℏ/mc. Below this line, quantum uncertainty prevents localization. Pair production creates new particles rather than allowing further confinement. Fundamental particles lie on this line.

The Instanton

At the intersection of both boundaries sits the Planck-mass instanton: a black hole whose Compton wavelength equals its Schwarzschild radius. It has Planck mass (~2.18 × 10-5 g) and Planck length (~1.62 × 10-33 cm). It is the smallest possible object.

Isodensity lines

Diagonal lines of slope 3 connect objects of equal density. Life, planets, and stars cluster near atomic density (~1 g/cm³). Neutron stars sit on nuclear density (~1014 g/cm³). The current Universe has critical density ~10-29 g/cm³.
